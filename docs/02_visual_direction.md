# PPM – Pro Performance Maintenance
## Visual Direction

---

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the visual direction for the new PPM – Pro Performance Maintenance website.

It should be used as the primary visual reference for:
- website layout design,
- UI components,
- art direction,
- photography generation,
- animation direction,
- visual consistency,
- section styling,
- image and video asset creation.

The goal is to create a website that feels premium, architectural, modern and trustworthy — not like a generic contractor or handyman website.

---

## 2. Core Visual Concept

The website should feel like:

> **Apple meets premium architectural contractor.**

This means the visual language should combine:
- the clarity and restraint of premium technology brands,
- the precision of architectural presentation,
- the trustworthiness of a professional property services company,
- the craft and texture of real painting, plastering and maintenance work.

The website should be minimal, refined and confident, while still showing real trade execution and practical workmanship.

---

## 3. Desired Visual Impression

When a visitor lands on the website, they should immediately feel:

- PPM is premium.
- PPM is organised.
- PPM is professional.
- PPM is detail-oriented.
- PPM works with high-value properties.
- PPM is suitable for both residential and commercial clients.
- PPM can be trusted by homeowners, builders, real estate agencies, property managers and strata managers.
- PPM delivers clean, precise and reliable work.

The site should communicate quality through spacing, typography, photography, motion and restraint — not through loud decorative effects.

---

Use the following keywords as the core design language:

## 5. What the Website Should Not Look Like

Avoid the following visual directions:

- generic handyman website,
- cheap painting contractor website,
- yellow/black construction branding,
- cluttered service website,
- generic SaaS landing page,
- overly corporate template,
- excessive stock-photo layouts,
- heavy gradients,
- cartoon icons,
- messy construction tape clichés,
- aggressive sales-page visuals,
- overloaded UI,
- loud badges and discount banners,
- fake luxury with gold gradients,
- chaotic before/after grids,
- low-quality AI-looking worker images.

The website should not look budget, generic or loud. It should feel controlled, premium and architectural.

## 8. Colour Rules

Follow these rules:

- Keep the palette restrained.
- Avoid excessive gradients.
- Avoid bright accent colours unless required for small functional UI states.
- Do not introduce yellow/black contractor colours.
- Do not use random blues outside the defined palette.
- Use contrast deliberately.
- Let whitespace and dark navy sections create the premium feeling.
- Use #000d20 as the main signature tone.
- Use #e9e9e9 and #ffffff to avoid a heavy, dark-only site.

---

## 9. Typography Direction

Typography should be modern, architectural and premium.

Recommended style:
- like gotham font
- clean geometric sans-serif,
- modern grotesk,
- strong editorial headings,
- refined body text,
- generous line-height,
- clear hierarchy,
- minimal decorative typography.

Potential font directions:
- Gotham
- premium sans-serif similar to Neue Haas Grotesk, Inter, Söhne, Helvetica Now, Suisse, Avenir Next, or similar,
- headings may use a slightly tighter, more architectural sans-serif,
- body text should remain highly readable.

Avoid:
- decorative script fonts,
- overly condensed construction fonts,
- playful fonts,
- cheap bold display fonts,
- generic template typography,
- too many font families.

---

## 10. Typography Hierarchy

Recommended hierarchy:

### H1
Large, editorial, confident.

Usage:
- hero headline,
- major positioning statements.

Style:
- strong weight,
- tight but readable letter spacing,
- generous line-height,
- premium spacing around it.

Example tone:
> Premium painting, finishing and maintenance for Sydney properties.

---

### H2
Section-defining, clean, architectural.

Usage:
- section titles,
- services,
- projects,
- why choose us,
- process,
- testimonials.

Style:
- clear,
- slightly smaller than H1,
- strong but not loud.

---

### H3
Component-level title.

Usage:
- service card titles,
- process steps,
- project cards,
- trust points.

---

### Body Text
Should be clear, calm and professional.

Style:
- medium line-height,
- easy to scan,
- no exaggerated marketing tone,
- avoid long paragraphs.

---

### Captions / Labels
Use small uppercase or refined technical labels.

Examples:
- SYDNEY, NSW
- RESIDENTIAL & COMMERCIAL
- PAINTING
- PLASTERING
- STRATA MAINTENANCE
- BEFORE / AFTER

Labels should feel architectural, not noisy.

---

## 11. Layout Direction

The layout should use:
- large whitespace,
- strong section rhythm,
- editorial composition,
- clear hierarchy,
- wide image areas,
- asymmetric but balanced layouts,
- architectural grids,
- premium spacing,
- strong contrast between dark and light sections.

The page should feel spacious, controlled and intentional.

---

## 12. Grid System

Use a modern web grid system.

Recommended:
- 12-column desktop grid,
- max-width around 1200–1440 px,
- generous side padding,
- mobile-first responsive scaling,
- consistent vertical rhythm.

Suggested breakpoints:
```text
Mobile: 360–480 px
Tablet: 768 px
Laptop: 1024–1280 px
Desktop: 1440 px
Large desktop: 1920 px+
```

Design should look premium on large screens, but remain highly usable on mobile.

---

## 13. Section Rhythm

The website should alternate between:
- dark premium navy sections,
- clean white/light-grey editorial sections,
- image-led service sections,
- before/after proof sections,
- refined CTA areas.

Avoid making every section look the same.

Recommended rhythm:
1. Strong dark or video-led hero.
2. Light trust/credibility strip.
3. Editorial brand positioning.
4. Services grid.
5. Paint roller transition.
6. Before/after projects.
7. Dark why-choose-us section.
8. Process section.
9. Testimonials/social proof.
10. Final CTA.
11. Contact/footer.

---

## 15. Worker Photography Rules

Workers should wear:
- navy workwear close to #081c33 or #000d20,
- PPM logo in white,
- clean but realistic trade clothing,
- gloves when appropriate,
- practical work pants,
- authentic tools.

Worker images should show real action:
- painting with roller,
- smoothing plaster with trowel,
- installing carpentry,
- repairing strata surfaces,
- patching/remedial work.

Avoid:
- fake-looking hands,
- distorted tools,
- unrealistic body poses,
- excessive dirt and chaos,
- overly staged stock poses,
- workers without branding,
- yellow/black construction branding,
- helmets unless the scene genuinely requires them.

## 20. Motion Direction

Animations should be subtle, premium and meaningful.

Use:
- scroll reveals,
- mask reveals,
- pinned section effects,
- before/after slider,
- paint roller scroll transition,
- soft parallax,
- image reveal through clip-path,
- sticky navigation shrink,
- refined hover states.

Avoid:
- bouncing,
- cartoon motion,
- excessive rotation,
- noisy particle effects,
- overanimated sections,
- heavy motion on mobile.

Motion should support the brand story:
- preparation,
- painting,
- smoothing,
- revealing,
- finishing,
- precision.

---

## 21. Paint Roller Interaction Direction

The paint roller effect is a signature interaction for PPM.

Visual direction:
- realistic roller,
- realistic paint strip,
- transparent PNG/WebP assets,
- deep navy paint colours,
- professional surface texture,
- premium controlled motion.

Use cases:
- section transitions,
- service separators,
- hero-to-services transition,
- before/after reveal,
- final CTA reveal.

Technical visual principle:
- roller and paint strip should be separate assets,
- strip should be revealed by mask/clip,
- roller should travel with scroll progress,
- effect should look like the roller is actually painting the page.

Do not overuse. Use as a memorable brand detail, not a gimmick.

---

## 22. Surface Texture Direction

Use texture carefully.

Allowed:
- subtle plaster texture,
- paint roller strip texture,
- architectural wall surface,
- fine concrete/plaster details,
- matte paint finish.

Avoid:
- grunge,
- dirty construction textures,
- excessive noise,
- chaotic overlays,
- strong paper textures,
- fake distressed effects.

Texture should communicate finishing quality, not mess.
